# Trialta Go-to-Market Assessment

# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Trialta is a technically strong advisory firm with credible experience across Transaction Services (TS) and CFO/Finance & Accounting (F\&A). The quality of work is not the issue. The constraint is that the firm does not operate a real go-to-market engine.

Today, revenue depends almost entirely on personal networks, past employer clients, and sporadic referrals. This model is survivable in a strong deal market but becomes fragile in a slower, more competitive one. Trialta isn’t losing opportunities because of capability—it’s losing them because the market doesn’t consistently see or grasp the firm’s value.

Together, these gaps create a firm that performs at a high level operationally but appears far smaller and less differentiated commercially

The root issue: Trialta has strong relationships, strong operators, and strong delivery—but no system to turn those strengths into predictable revenue.

At its core, Trialta’s commercial challenge is simple: strong operators, strong work, weak systems. This combination produces inconsistent visibility and unpredictable growth. Across all interviews, four themes were clear:

1. ### Visibility is low.

   Bankers and PE deal teams simply don’t see or remember the firm often enough to drive repeatable deal flow.

2. ### Differentiation is unclear.

   Trialta’s strongest attributes—seniority, speed, judgment, ability to work through imperfect data—are not shown effectively in messaging, website, or collateral.

3. ### Commercial infrastructure is missing.

   There is no CRM foundation, no follow-up system, no nurture mechanism, no consistent content, no structured outreach, and no GTM operating rhythm.

4. ### TS and CFO/F\&A are not integrated into one commercial story.

   CFO/F\&A is a new service line with clear demand—but without structured positioning, a public launch, and a TS→CFO expansion motion, it cannot generate meaningful visibility or pipeline.

Despite these gaps, the foundation is strong. Trialta has senior operators, trusted relationships, relevant vertical depth, and a CFO/F\&A offering the market clearly wants. With even a modest GTM system, deal flow, win rates, and revenue predictability can increase meaningfully.

The recommended path includes four sequential phases:

* 0–30 Days: Build GTM infrastructure – CRM, target list, outreach cadences, messaging, website credibility fixes, CFO/F\&A launch, and clear GTM ownership.  
* 30–90 Days: Build the GTM engine – pitch deck, one-pagers, case studies, partner LinkedIn visibility, webinars, and a defined sales process.  
* 90–180 Days: Strengthen repeatability – TS→CFO expansion motion, enhanced proposals, brand/website refresh, and ongoing case proof.  
* 180+ Days: Scale authority – M\&A roundtables, CFO events, a periodic newsletter, annual insights piece, and continued credibility building.

If Trialta does not fix these issues, growth will remain inconsistent and overly dependent on individual relationships. If they do fix them, the firm becomes a visible, credible, top-of-mind boutique—with stronger margins, more inbound opportunities, higher win rates, and a far more predictable revenue engine.

Fixing the GTM engine should realistically produce higher deal flow volume and visibility over the next 12 months.

# BUSINESS MODEL & REVENUE CONTEXT

Trialta is a boutique professional services firm with two primary lines of business:

1. Transaction Services (TS) – Financial due diligence (FDD) and quality of earnings (QoE) work for private equity, bankers, and occasionally corporate acquirers.  
2. CFO / Finance & Accounting Services (F\&A) – Project-based and interim support for F\&A teams, including financial reporting, technical accounting, close acceleration, post-close integration, audit prep, and broader operational finance needs.

Below is the real, unvarnished picture of how the business works today.

## What the Firm Really Sells (Not Just the Services)

Trialta sells expertise, not hours.

Clients buy them because:

* They trust the team for its senior-level judgment and practical conclusions—not caveats or guesswork.  
* They know the operators personally and prefer calling experienced practitioners over working with a faceless, leveraged team.  
* They rely on Trialta to make sense of imperfect data—diving deep, filling gaps, and turning messy details into a clear investor narrative.  
* They know Trialta will execute quickly, adapt as the deal shifts, and respond in real time when issues surface.  
* They want to avoid Big Four premiums for work typically pushed down to juniors who lack deal context.  
* They value that senior people do the work end-to-end—no handoffs, no dilution of expertise.

This matters because Trialta’s GTM and pricing should reflect the value created by seasoned experts—not the hours consumed by analysts.

Right now, the market doesn’t consistently see this difference, which means Trialta’s strongest advantage is not being communicated.

## Revenue Model: Referrals, Relationships, and Rolodex

Today, revenue is driven almost exclusively by:

1. “People we’ve worked with before” – Mentioned repeatedly and explicitly in the transcripts.  
2. Warm introductions through former colleagues – Often from past firms or adjacent deal partners.  
3. PE deal teams who already trust them – Particularly repeat deal activity.  
4. PortCo relationships – Especially where the CFO or controller is familiar with the team.  
5. Light inbound referrals – Usually low-quality (“we assumed you’d be cheap because you’re small”) and price-sensitive.  
6. Opportunistic banker intros – Inconsistent and often forgotten due to lack of follow-up.

This isn’t a criticism—this is the typical starting point for TS firms. But it is not scalable and it is highly fragile in a slower deal environment.

## Revenue Concentration Patterns

1. The 80/20 dynamic is present – A few highly reputable PE sponsors drive a large share of volume.  
2. CFO services revenue could be opportunistic, not consistent – The CFO/F\&A practice can cross-sell into existing TS accounts, but this is not structured yet and will depend heavily on timing and personal rapport.  
3. Corporate buyers (non-PE) are infrequent and inconsistent – The team views them as unpredictable and hard to target.  
4. Banker-led revenue is minimal – Because bankers are transactional and rely heavily on recent interactions, Trialta’s lack of consistent, proactive outreach keeps them off the banker “short list,” which directly reduces deal flow.

## Current Commercial Reality

### A. They excel at “1 → 5”. They struggle at “0 → 1.”

Sam said this most clearly: “I feel very confident getting from 1 to 5\. The trick is getting from 0 to 1 with a client you actually want.” 

This is the single most important line across all transcripts.

It sums up Trialta’s business model today: If someone already trusts them, they will reuse them forever. If someone doesn’t know them, there is almost no chance they will discover, remember, or choose them.

That is not a GTM system—that is a referral dependency.

### B. High-value clients are subsidized by low-value ones

Repeatedly, the team mentions:

* low-fee projects  
* price-sensitive clients  
* time-consuming small deals  
* cost complaints  
* market “race to the bottom” dynamics

The team knows they should walk away from bad-fit deals, but you can only walk away when your top-of-funnel is strong enough.

Right now, it’s not—so they can’t.

### C. Deal cycles depend on who remembers them

When Trialta is remembered, they get the call. When they are not, they don’t.

There is no outbound motion to counteract this. There is no nurture motion. There is no sequencing or CRM to maintain relationships.

The result is a revenue model highly vulnerable to:

* deal market slowdowns  
* banker turnover  
* PE team changes  
* lack of visibility  
* inconsistent outreach

This would concern any investor or CEO because it makes revenue unpredictable.

## Pricing Reality

Trialta’s pricing is fair for their expertise—but the market often treats them as a low-cost boutique because:

* they lack clear differentiation  
* their web presence does not validate premium perception  
* their collateral does not show value  
* their brand story is muted  
* many prospects assume small firms \= cheaper

This leads to:

* frequent price objections  
* mismatched expectations  
* unqualified inbounds  
* stress on utilization and margins

Ironically, the firm’s real differentiator (hands-on senior expertise) should justify premium pricing—but the market never hears this story.

## Profitability Levers

The firm’s profitability improves dramatically when:

* deals come from high-quality repeat PE  
* CFO/FA work is embedded long-term  
* pricing is anchored to deal complexity  
* cross-sell occurs post-TS project  
* they avoid bad-fit, low-price one-offs

But right now:

* follow-up is inconsistent  
* collateral is weak  
* outreach is sporadic  
* CRM is absent  
* marketing is nonexistent  
* differentiation is unclear

So the business is not capturing its full margin potential.

## Growth Levers Available (But Underutilized)

Trialta’s unrealized growth levers are:

1. Deepening wallet share with existing PE funds – They currently serve 6–7 PortCos at funds that have 20+. That is a massive opportunity.  
2. Systematic banker outreach – Bankers forget everyone who doesn’t stay top-of-mind.  
3. Packaging CFO/F\&A into clear offer structures – Their capabilities are strong—but not marketed.  
4. LinkedIn presence (individual \+ firm) – Courtney explicitly mentioned this is a proven channel.  
5. Industry POV content – Particularly for beauty, consumer, retail, and light tech—where the team has real experience.  
6. A CRM-driven nurturing system – A CRM will materially change their commercial discipline.

## Summary Diagnosis

Trialta’s business model has high capability and low market visibility. It has high client trust but low scalability. It offers premium expertise but commands inconsistent premium pricing.

Revenue growth is capped not by the market, but by:

* lack of infrastructure  
* lack of consistent outreach  
* lack of positioning  
* lack of systemized GTM behavior

The firm is fundamentally strong—but commercially underbuilt.

# GTM GAPS, RISKS, & CONSTRAINTS

This section identifies the biggest structural weaknesses in Trialta’s commercial engine—not symptoms, but the underlying constraints that create inconsistent revenue, weak visibility, and a fragile pipeline.

## Gap 1—Overreliance on Personal Networks (Non-Scalable Revenue Model)

**Diagnosis:**  
Trialta’s revenue is driven overwhelmingly by the personal networks of its partners. Deals originate almost exclusively from warm intros, with little new-logo acquisition. This creates an unpredictable, fragile revenue engine.

**Why this matters:**

* If even one network weakens, deal volume drops immediately.  
* The firm must accept lower-value, lower-margin work to stay busy.  
* You cannot scale or forecast a business built on relationship luck.

## Gap 2—No CRM or Relationship Operating System

**Diagnosis:**  
Relationships are managed through memory and scattered notes. No contact system, no follow-up visibility, no pipeline view, and no way to measure activity. Trialta loses opportunities not because of quality, but because follow-up is inconsistent.

**Why this matters:**

* Missed follow-up \= lost deals.  
* Potential clients forget firms that don’t show up consistently.  
* GTM cannot be scaled or delegated without a CRM backbone.

## Gap 3—Weak Collateral (Does Not Match Quality of Work)

**Diagnosis:**  
Trialta lacks the foundational assets buyers expect in TS and CFO/F\&A: pitch decks, one-pagers, banker packs, tombstones, case studies, vertical pages, and product sheets. The market has no easy way to understand the firm’s capability.

**Why this matters:**

* Bankers cannot easily advocate for Trialta internally.  
* Mid-funnel conversion suffers because buyers lack proof.  
* The firm appears smaller and less experienced than it is.

## Gap 4—Undefined Positioning & Messaging (No Differentiation)

**Diagnosis:**  
Trialta does not consistently articulate who they are, what they do, why they’re different, or when to hire them. Each team member tells a slightly different story. Website copy is generic and does not reflect seniority or operator mindset.

**Why this matters:**

* Buyers default to price when firms sound interchangeable.  
* “Boutique \= cheaper” perception remains unchallenged.  
* No messaging foundation exists to support outreach, website updates, or sales enablement.

## Gap 5—Market Visibility & Outbound Weakness

**Diagnosis:**  
Trialta is effectively invisible to the market. There is no structured outbound rhythm, no nurture, no recurrence, no social presence, and no system for staying top-of-mind. PE and bankers hear from them only when someone “remembers” to reach out.

**Why this matters:**

* Firms that show up consistently get the call; Trialta doesn’t.  
* Pipeline is unpredictable and reliant on luck.  
* Demand for CFO/F\&A or new TS work cannot grow without visibility.

## Gap 6—No Cross-Practice Revenue System (TS ↔ CFO/F\&A)

**Diagnosis:**  
TS and CFO/F\&A risk operating as separate silos with no structured lifecycle progression, no handoffs, and no predictable expansion engine. CFO/F\&A is a new practice, but lacks the commercial infrastructure to benefit from TS deal flow.

**Why this matters:**

* Trialta leaves meaningful expansion revenue untouched.  
* Stickiness remains low when clients only buy one service.  
* PE lifecycle (TS → Integration → CFO/F\&A) is not leveraged.

## Gap 7—No Formal Sales Process

**Diagnosis:**  
The team is excellent in conversation but lacks the systems around the conversation: qualification criteria, discovery frameworks, proposal templates, objection handling, pipeline hygiene, and stage definitions.

**Why this matters:**

* Deals die quietly after first call.  
* Trialta cannot forecast or manage a real pipeline.  
* Operators shoulder unnecessary cognitive load during the sales process.

## Gap 8—External Credibility Does Not Match Capability

**Diagnosis:**  
The website, sales collateral, and public proof points make Trialta appear young, small, and lightly experienced. Tombstones are absent, case studies are absent, and the site does not reflect the firm’s actual seniority, deal sophistication, or capabilities.

**Why this matters:**

* Buyers’ first impression suppresses pricing power.  
* Credibility deficit hurts competitive processes.  
* Website undermines strong in-person conversations.

## Summary: The Root-Cause Pattern

Trialta’s challenges are not capability problems—they are system problems.

The firm relies on operator skill and personal networks instead of a scalable GTM engine. It delivers high-quality work, but the market cannot see, understand, or remember that value. It wins in conversation, but lacks the commercial infrastructure to win consistently.

Building the GTM engine fixes the root cause and creates repeatable, predictable growth.

# PRIORITIZED RECOMMENDATIONS

The path forward is staged and designed to actually get done in a firm where partners are also operators. Each phase fixes one layer of the commercial engine so the next layer can work.

The structure: 0–30 Days → 30–90 Days → 90–180 Days → 180+ Days. This is the shortest path from invisible → visible, opportunistic → systematic, plateaued → scalable.

## Guiding Principles (Read First)

1\. Visibility drives deal flow.  
Clients hire the firms they remember—not always the firms that are best.

2\. Systems before scale.  
Outreach, nurture, and expansion only work when CRM, segmentation, and ownership exist.

3\. Proof beats claims.  
Tombstones, case studies, POVs, and diagnostics build more trust than any positioning statement.

4\. Cross-practice leverage multiplies revenue.  
TS → CFO/F\&A is the lifecycle that stabilizes and grows the firm.

5\. Small motions, done consistently, beat ambitious motions inconsistently executed.  
This roadmap is intentionally realistic for a partner-operator environment.

6\. One owner per workstream.  
Shared ownership guarantees execution failure.

## 0–30 Days—Build GTM Infrastructure (Highest Urgency)

Goal: Stop relationship leakage and establish the minimum viable commercial system.

### Implement HubSpot Correctly (Non-Negotiable Priority \#1)

Trialta cannot scale outreach, nurturing, or follow-up without a real CRM foundation. Everything else depends on this being implemented cleanly.

If HubSpot is wrong, every GTM motion downstream fails—this is the system the whole engine runs on.

Trialta needs one unified, centralized list of the people who drive deals. This becomes the backbone of all outreach, nurturing, and visibility.

Actions

* 40–60 PE deal leaders

You can’t build consistent deal flow if you don’t know exactly who you’re trying to stay in front of.

Actions

Bankers and PE won’t remember Trialta unless the firm stays in front of them—consistency beats volume.
